Myth or Maxxed?

Three things men get told about their looks. One isn't true.

Looksmaxxing advice spreads faster than it gets checked. Tap each claim to see whether the evidence backs it, complicates it, or kills it.

"Drink tons of water and your skin will glow." Tap to check ↓
Half-truth

Dehydration does make skin look duller and more sunken — so if you're actually under-drinking, fixing that helps. But once you're adequately hydrated, drinking more doesn't give extra "glow." Skin moisture is mostly controlled by your barrier and environment, not how many litres you pour in. Drink to thirst; don't expect a litre to replace moisturiser.

"Shaving your beard makes it grow back thicker." Tap to check ↓
Myth

This one's been tested and it's false. Shaving cuts the hair at the surface — it doesn't touch the follicle, which is what determines thickness and growth rate. Regrowth feels coarser only because the cut leaves a blunt tip instead of a tapered one. It looks darker against the skin, but the hair itself is unchanged.

"Sleeping earlier actually changes how your face looks." Tap to check ↓
Backed by evidence

This one holds up. Poor sleep is linked to more visible under-eye darkness, puffiness, paler skin and drooping corners of the mouth — people literally rate sleep-deprived faces as more tired and less healthy. It also raises cortisol, which works against skin repair. Not vanity: it's one of the few free, repeatable inputs that genuinely moves the needle.
